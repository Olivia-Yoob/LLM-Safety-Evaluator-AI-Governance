# Anthropic Usage Policy Mapping

**Purpose.** Translate the academic Jigsaw 6-category toxicity taxonomy into the
vocabulary a frontier-AI safety team actually governs in — the **Anthropic Usage
Policy** ("Universal Usage Standards"). This is the connective artefact that turns a
Kaggle dataset into a *governable safety control*.

> The Anthropic Usage Policy clauses below are **paraphrased** from the public policy at
> `anthropic.com/legal/aup`. They are an analyst's mapping for portfolio purposes, not a
> verbatim reproduction or an official Anthropic position. Verify against the live policy
> before operational use.

## Why the mapping is the project's spine

The `severity_tier` column is the load-bearing element. It propagates through the whole
pipeline as a single governance lever:

- **Notebook 3 (Claude API):** the policy clause becomes the classifier's *target
  vocabulary* — Claude judges against `Do Not Incite … Violence`, not the bare token
  `threat`. The system prompt is built directly from this map
  (`src.llm_client.build_system_prompt`), so prompt and mapping cannot drift apart.
- **Notebook 4 (Red team):** severity prioritises adversarial effort — `Critical`
  harms (`threat`, `identity_hate`) get the hardest probes (veiled threats, implicit
  coded hate).
- **Notebook 5 (Governance):** severity sets **differential decision thresholds**
  (Critical = lower threshold = higher recall) and **differential drift tolerances**
  (Critical categories trip escalation on the smallest degradation).

| Severity tier | Categories | Decision threshold | Drift tolerance (recall) | Rationale |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Critical | `threat`, `identity_hate` | 0.30 | 0.03 | A missed harm is a real-world safety / discrimination event |
| High | `severe_toxic` | 0.40 | 0.05 | Intense abuse; serious but less safety-critical than violence |
| Medium | `toxic`, `obscene`, `insult` | 0.50 | 0.08 | Standard moderation; over-flagging cost is comparatively high |

## Mapping caveats (intellectual honesty)

1. **Not 1:1.** Anthropic's policy taxonomy and Jigsaw's are different ontologies; the
   mapping is a *best-fit* analyst judgement, not a bijection. `obscene`, for instance,
   spans both adult-content and harassment depending on context.
2. **Policy evolves.** Usage policies are updated; this mapping is a point-in-time
   snapshot and should be re-validated against the live policy.
3. **Labels ≠ truth.** Jigsaw labels are crowd-sourced and carry annotation bias; the
   mapping inherits any such bias.
4. **Severity tiers are a design choice.** The tiering reflects a defensible risk
   ranking (violence/discrimination > intense abuse > general incivility) but a
   different organisation might tier differently — the *mechanism* is the contribution,
   not the specific numbers.
